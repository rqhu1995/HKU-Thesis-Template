    Based on the user-defined rebalancing schedule (in rebalancing specifications), the current bike inventory and dock information
of the system is passed to the optimization module by the rebalancing controller. The optimization module generates route and operation
decisions for each vehicle to meet the user-defined service level requirements using the heuristic presented in Section 3.4. Then, all
the rebalancing tasks are sent to the simulation module to execute the rebalancing and to continue mimicking the operation of the
BSS until the next rebalancing starts. To evaluate the performance of the proposed heuristic, the simulation module calculates the
true average ratio of satisfied demand over total demand (i.e., service level) for both bike pickup/return of the BSS at the end of
each rebalancing.

3.2. Simulation improvements

3.2.1. Bike borrowing and returning
    The bike borrowing and returning starts with creating bike pick-up demand using a non-homogeneous Poisson process (NHPP)
with piece-wise constant arrival rates. Noted that the bike pick-up demand is time and station dependent. The time dependency is
modeled by assuming a behavior that changes every hour and every day of a week. Specifically, the bike pick-up demand of each
station is estimated by the average number of arrivals in any given hour in a week. Compared to the basic model provided in Jin
et al. [9], this improved simulation model is able to provide valet service for the high-volume stations at peak hours. When a bike
pick-up demand is generated at a start station, the user will first ask the valet for a bike before picking up one from the docks of
the station. Then a bike will be assigned to the user if the start station or the valet at the start station has available bikes. The valet
inventory capacity is based on a user-defined percentage of the station capacity. At the end of the valet’s shift, all the bikes that the
valet holds are returned to the depot. The improved bike borrowing and the returning process is illustrated in Fig. 2.
    After receiving a bike, the user selects a destination based on a discrete distribution over the identification numbers of the
stations in the system. The probability of selecting a destination station 𝑗 is estimated as an empirical probability that a user goes
from the start station 𝑖 to station 𝑗 at any given hour and day of a week (i.e., the number of trips from 𝑖 to 𝑗 over the total number
of trips starting at 𝑖). After determining the destination station, the corresponding trip duration is modeled by a shifted Gamma
distribution. Ideally, a unique Gamma distribution can be estimated based on the historical trips for each pair of stations. However,
when the trip records of some stations are insufficient, the trip duration will be modeled by the Gamma distribution based on the
system average. An example of such gamma distribution estimated by using the 2019 trip data is provided in Fig. 3.
    Similar to the bike pick-up process, the user first asks the valet for returning a bike upon arrival at the destination station. If no
docks are available, the user goes to the nearest station with available docks. After the bike is returned, the age (i.e., the cumulative
usage) of the bike is updated based on the trip duration. The older the bike is, the higher the failure probability will be. Moreover,
the bike’s age will be reset to zero after it gets repaired.
    The bike condition is updated to fail if its current age is larger than the age of the next failure. The age of the next failure can be
estimated based on the reliability function of bikes. Dock failures are generated by conducting a Bernoulli trial when the user tries
to pick up a bike at the station. Currently published data sets lack information of broken bikes or docks [6]. For docks, we assume
a small probability of failure. On the other hand, a bike failure can be reasonably identified by filtering trip record satisfying: (1)
the trip starts and ends in the same station, and (2) the trip duration is extremely short (e.g., less than two minutes). In practice,
customers might not notice that a bike is broken until they take it out of the dock and try to ride it. Therefore, in the historical trip
data, many trips have shown the two features. Using the trip data of Citi Bike as an example, there are 12,007 records showing this
pattern in August 2019, which accounts for 92% of the reported failures for the same period.
