\title{A Cluster-First, Route-Second Decomposition Method for the Static Bike Repositioning Problem with Broken Bikes and On-Site Repair}
\author{Runqiu Hu and W.Y. Szeto}

\begin{abstract}
This paper addresses the static bike-repositioning problem with broken bikes
and on-site repair, a routing and inventory-planning problem that arises in
connection with docked bike-sharing systems. Bike-sharing is a green
transportation mode that supports short-distance trips and connections to
public transport. One major challenge is that the demand for and supply of
usable bikes and vacant docks are not always matched. This challenge becomes
more difficult when broken bikes are present, because broken bikes cannot be
rented and may occupy docks needed by users. Hence, trucks are used to move
usable bikes and collect broken bikes, and repairers are used to repair broken
bikes at stations. In this paper, we propose an EUDF-aware cluster-first
route-second heuristic for solving the problem. A hybrid genetic search is
developed to form station clusters, and an Extended Activated Transfer
Selection procedure is used to estimate the reduction in user dissatisfaction
within each cluster. The resulting cluster-level routing problems are solved by
the HGSADC-SBC heuristic. The heuristic is evaluated on Citi Bike-based
instances with up to 2000 stations. The results of computational experiments
indicate that the heuristic obtains better feasible objective values than the
Gurobi upper bounds on small instances and the monolithic HGSADC-SBC heuristic
on both small and large instances. The average improvement over the monolithic
heuristic is 7.26\% on the small instances and 8.81\% on the large instances,
and it reaches 11.71\% on the 2000-station instances.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Bike repositioning problem; Broken bikes; On-site repair; User dissatisfaction function; Cluster-first route-second; Hybrid genetic search

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Bike-sharing systems (BSSs) have become an important component of urban
mobility, providing short-distance trips and first- and last-mile connections
to public transport. A persistent operational difficulty in these systems is
the spatial and temporal imbalance between bike and dock
availability. During commuting peaks, stations in residential areas may be
depleted of bikes, while stations near employment centers may become nearly
full. Users arriving at an empty station cannot rent a bike, and users arriving
at a full station cannot return one. Operators therefore regularly redistribute
bikes across stations to improve the availability of both usable bikes and
vacant docks. Determining vehicle routes and station-level loading and
unloading quantities for this redistribution is known as the Bike
Repositioning Problem (BRP).

The static BRP is commonly performed during off-peak periods, often overnight,
when user activity is assumed to be negligible and station inventories change
only through operator interventions. In many formulations that do not consider
broken bikes, each station is represented by a single inventory level, and a
fleet of repositioning trucks picks up bikes from stations with surplus
inventory and delivers bikes to stations with deficit inventory. A typical
solution specifies the sequence of stations visited by each truck and the
number of bikes picked up or delivered at each visited station. This setting has provided
the basis for many static BRP models and algorithms, but it does not fully
capture the operational conditions of real BSSs with broken bikes.

In practice, a portion of the bikes in a system may be broken. Broken bikes
alter the repositioning problem in two ways. First, they
cannot satisfy rental demand. A station with many bikes may still provide poor
service if most of them are broken. Second, in docked systems, broken bikes
occupy docks and can prevent users from returning usable bikes. The
operational relevance of this issue has been documented in real systems. For
example, Barcelona reported that about 12\% of shared bikes were severely
damaged and about 55\% were lightly damaged, while in New York, about 2\% of
bikes required major repairs every day
\citep{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025,kaspiDetectionUnusableBicycles2016}.
These observations motivate the study of the BRP with broken bikes, where
repositioning of usable bikes and handling of broken bikes must be planned
together.

Existing studies on BRPs with broken bikes have considered several forms of
maintenance integration. Some studies focus on collecting broken bikes and
transporting them to depots, repair centers, or bike shops for off-site repair
\citep{wangStaticGreenRepositioning2018,wangEnhancedArtificialBee2021}.
Others schedule maintenance crews or repair tasks separately from the main
repositioning operation \citep{luTaskAssignmentPredictive2022}. Another study
considers repair-before-repositioning schemes, where light-fault bikes are
repaired on site before rebalancing vehicles operate
\citep{zhangBikeRebalancingMaintenance2022}. More recently,
\citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025} introduced a static BRP with broken
bikes and on-site repairers, in which truck-based repositioning and
broken-bike collection are jointly planned with repairer-based on-site repair.
In that setting, trucks and repairers form two independently routed fleets
operating within the same repositioning horizon. Trucks may move usable bikes
and collect broken bikes, while repairers may visit stations and repair broken
bikes directly in the field. The model decides truck routes, repairer routes,
truck loading and unloading quantities, and repair quantities at visited
stations.

These decisions determine the final station states that users experience after
repositioning, so the next issue is how the service quality of a repositioning
plan should be evaluated. Many static BRP formulations specify a target
inventory level or target interval for each station and penalize the deviation
of the post-repositioning inventory from that target
\citep{benchimolBalancingStationsSelf2011,schuijbroekInventoryRebalancingVehicle2017}.
These deviation-based measures are based on deviation from a desired inventory
state. This representation is useful for inventory balancing, but it does not
model how station-specific rental and return demand processes over the
planning horizon translate a post-repositioning inventory into expected rental
and return failures.
\citet{ravivOptimalInventoryManagement2013} introduced the User Dissatisfaction
Function (UDF), which uses station-specific rental and return demand over a
planning horizon to estimate the expected number of failed rentals and returns
at a station as a function of its post-repositioning inventory. The UDF is
station-specific and convex over the feasible inventory levels, allowing
service improvements to be evaluated in terms of expected user dissatisfaction
rather than inventory deviations.

The UDF represents station state by a single inventory level. For BRPs with
broken bikes, however, station state includes usable-bike and broken-bike
inventories. \citet{kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017} extended the UDF to the
Extended User Dissatisfaction Function (EUDF), which is defined over
usable-bike and broken-bike inventories. The EUDF provides a natural
service-quality measure for BRPs with broken bikes because its station-level
state includes both inventories.

While EUDF-based modeling improves the representation of service quality,
solving BRPs with broken bikes and on-site repair remains computationally
challenging. The static BRP is NP-hard
\citep{benchimolBalancingStationsSelf2011}, and incorporating the handling of
broken bikes and on-site repair expands the set of decisions. A BRP plan
includes both visit sequences and station-level quantity decisions, so a
monolithic solution of the full city-wide problem can become computationally
expensive as network size grows. This motivates decomposition methods that
partition the station network before constructing BRP plans for smaller
subproblems.

A common way to improve scalability in large-scale BRPs is to use a
cluster-first route-second (CFRS) structure. In a CFRS framework, the station
network is first partitioned into smaller clusters, and BRP subproblems are
then solved within or across these clusters. This decomposition can reduce
computational burden and allow cluster-level BRP plans to be constructed
independently or in parallel. Prior CFRS-based BRP studies have used spatial
proximity \citep{huangStaticBikeRepositioning2020,lvHybridAlgorithmStatic2020},
service-level feasibility constraints
\citep{schuijbroekInventoryRebalancingVehicle2017}, feature-correlation
information \citep{lvFeatureCorrelationReinforce2024}, and data-driven demand
and quantity information \citep{liuDataDrivenOptimizationFramework2025} to
guide the clustering phase. These criteria are useful for controlling travel
cost and ensuring basic operational feasibility, but they do not directly
evaluate the reduction in expected user dissatisfaction produced by a cluster.

Recent UDF-aware CFRS work considers user dissatisfaction in the clustering
phase by evaluating the potential UDF reduction achievable within each cluster
\citep{hu2026userdissatisfactionaware}. In that framework, station
partitioning is guided by both routing-time feasibility and the estimated UDF
reduction from moving bikes between surplus and deficit stations. The
clustering criterion is therefore linked to the UDF-based objective used in the
BRP subproblem. Earlier work by \citet{forma3stepMathHeuristic2015} also
incorporated UDF reduction into a CFRS matheuristic. These studies demonstrate
the value of considering user dissatisfaction in the clustering phase rather
than relying only on spatial or demand-based partitioning.

For the BRP with broken bikes and on-site repair, the scaling challenge is how
to adapt the CFRS clustering criterion from a one-dimensional UDF setting to a
bivariate EUDF setting. Existing UDF-aware CFRS frameworks evaluate candidate
clusters through changes in a single inventory state. In the EUDF setting, a
cluster evaluator must account for changes in both usable-bike and broken-bike
inventories when estimating user dissatisfaction. The difficulty is not only
that the objective has two inventory dimensions, but also that repair and
collection actions can change these dimensions in coupled ways: on-site repair
converts a broken bike into a usable bike, while broken-bike collection changes
dock occupation without moving a usable bike. However, a CFRS framework for
large BRPs with broken bikes and on-site repair in which cluster formation and
cluster-level BRP planning both use the bivariate EUDF is currently lacking.

This paper addresses this gap by developing an EUDF-aware CFRS framework for
the static BRP with broken bikes and on-site repair. The framework decomposes
the city-wide problem into a clustering stage and a cluster-level planning
stage. The clustering stage partitions stations according to estimated EUDF
reduction under truck and repairer budgets, while the cluster-level planning
stage constructs BRP plans within each cluster. The truck and repairer fleets
are routed independently, but their effects are evaluated jointly through the
final inventories of usable and broken bikes at each station. To make the
clustering stage operational, the framework uses an evaluator that estimates
the combined effect of moving usable bikes, collecting broken bikes, and
repairing broken bikes on site before cluster-level BRP plans are constructed.
The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We develop an EUDF-aware CFRS framework for the static BRP with
    broken bikes and on-site repair, extending UDF-aware CFRS from a
    single-inventory setting to a bivariate EUDF setting;
    \item We introduce Extended Activated Transfer Selection (EATS), a scoring
    mechanism for candidate clusters that estimates EUDF reduction from moving
    usable bikes, collecting broken bikes, and repairing broken bikes on site
    under truck and repairer budgets; and
    \item We develop Hybrid Genetic Search for Extended-UDF Clustering
    (HGS-EUC), which uses EATS to solve the EUDF-aware station partitioning
    problem and is evaluated against monolithic HGS and CFRS comparators.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~2 formulates the
static BRP with broken bikes and on-site repair. Section~3 presents the
EUDF-aware CFRS framework and the EATS cluster evaluator. Section~4 describes
the HGS-EUC algorithm and the cluster-level BRP planning procedure. Section~5
reports the computational experiments and sensitivity analyses. Section~6
concludes the paper.

\section{Problem definition and model formulation}\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Problem setting}

This section presents the Bike Repositioning Problem with Broken Bikes and
On-Site Repair (BRP-BR-OSR), which is used as the reference problem in the
subsequent methodology. The problem is adapted from
\citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}: it retains the operational setting and
feasibility constraints of that model, removes the emission component, and
evaluates a repositioning plan by the total user dissatisfaction at all stations
on the following day.
% src: Model Formulation row huRepositioningBikeSharing2025

The problem considers a BSS with one depot and a finite set of stations over an
overnight rebalancing horizon. Each station has a fixed capacity, an initial
usable-bike inventory, and an initial broken-bike inventory, all of which are
assumed to be known at the beginning of the horizon. Usable bikes can be rented
by users after repositioning, whereas broken bikes cannot be rented and continue
to occupy docks until they are collected or repaired.

A fleet of repositioning trucks and a set of repairers operate within the same
time budget. Trucks depart from the depot, travel among stations, deliver or
collect usable bikes, collect broken bikes, may return intermediately to the
depot to load or unload bikes, and finally return to the depot. Repairers also
depart from the depot, visit stations with broken bikes, repair broken bikes
on site, and return to the depot. Each loading, unloading, or on-site repair
activity has a fixed processing time per bike. Trucks may visit stations and the
depot multiple times, while each station can be visited by at most one repairer.

The depot is assumed to have sufficient usable-bike inventory and sufficient
storage capacity for usable and broken bikes. Repairers only perform repairs
on-site at stations; the model does not decide how broken bikes returned to the
depot are repaired afterward. During the rebalancing horizon, no rental or return
demand occurs, and the inventories after repositioning become the starting
inventories for the following day. Following
\citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}, we assume that repairers working at
stations and any repair staff at the depot are paid fixed daily salaries.
Therefore, total repair staffing cost is constant for the day and independent of
the number of broken bikes repaired on site or sent back to the depot during the
repositioning period; it is not included in the objective.

The objective of the BRP-BR-OSR is to determine truck routes, repairer routes,
truck loading and unloading quantities, and on-site repair quantities that
minimize total user dissatisfaction at all stations on the following day, subject
to inventory, capacity, routing, and time-budget constraints.

Travel times and the rebalancing budget $T$ are measured in the same units.
To use them in the time-indexed formulation, we divide the horizon into periods
of equal length $\tau$ and convert truck and repairer travel times into discrete
period counts as follows:
\begin{align}
&t'_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
\left\lceil t_{ij}^{\mathrm{v}}/\tau \right\rceil, & i\ne j,\\
1, & i=j,
\end{cases}
\quad &&\forall i,j\in S_0, \label{eq:truck-periods}\\
&t''_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
\left\lceil t_{ij}^{\mathrm{r}}/\tau \right\rceil, & i\ne j,\\
1, & i=j,
\end{cases}
\quad &&\forall i,j\in S_0, \label{eq:repairer-periods}\\
&T' = \left\lfloor T/\tau \right\rfloor. && \label{eq:num-periods}
\end{align}
The binary route variables use a departure-period convention. If
$x_{i,j,t,k}^{\mathrm{v}}=1$, truck $k$ leaves node $i$ in period $t$ and can
next depart from node $j$ only after $t'_{ij}$ periods; repairer movements use
the same convention with $t''_{ij}$. These period counts maintain the discrete
route sequence. Original travel times and processing times are checked
separately by the cumulative time constraints in~\eqref{eq:time-feasibility}.
The ceiling operation in~\eqref{eq:truck-periods}--\eqref{eq:repairer-periods}
can overstate travel time within the time-expanded route; the remaining period
slack can absorb loading, unloading, and repair activities subject to the
cumulative time bounds. This time-indexed representation is used because trucks
may visit the same station or the depot multiple times, so the formulation must
track resource locations, station inventories, and truck loads over the
discretized horizon.

\subsection{EUDF-based objective}\label{sec:bivariate-udf}

Following \citet{kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017}, the EUDF value
$F_i(p_i,b_i)$ represents the weighted expected user dissatisfaction at station
$i$ over a day, given that $p_i$ usable bikes and $b_i$ broken bikes are present
at the station at the beginning of that day. In the BRP-BR-OSR, the overnight
repositioning plan determines the inventories after repositioning, which are used
as the starting inventories for the following day.
% src: Model Formulation row kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017

The feasible bivariate inventory states at station $i$ are
\begin{equation}
\Theta_i=\{(p_i,b_i)\in\mathbb{Z}_+^2:p_i+b_i\le C_i\}.
\label{eq:eudf-domain}
\end{equation}
The EUDF values are precomputed for all states in $\Theta_i$ and are treated as
model inputs. For the detailed calculation of these values, readers can refer to
\citet{kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017}.

\citet{kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017} also proposed a method for determining
a convex polyhedral function that closely approximates the EUDF values, enabling
the integration of the EUDF into the subsequent linear optimization model.
Specifically, for each $(p_i,b_i)\in\Theta_i$, a plane
$\alpha^i_{p_i,b_i}p_i+\beta^i_{p_i,b_i}b_i+\gamma^i_{p_i,b_i}$ can be fitted
as close as possible to $F_i(p_i,b_i)$, where
$\alpha^i_{p_i,b_i}$, $\beta^i_{p_i,b_i}$, and $\gamma^i_{p_i,b_i}$ are the
coefficients of the fitted plane for state $(p_i,b_i)$. These fitted-plane
coefficients are precomputed and used in the time-indexed MILP below.

\subsection{Time-indexed mixed-integer formulation}\label{sec:milp}

The time-indexed MILP for the BRP-BR-OSR is given as follows:
\begin{align}
\min \quad & \sum_{i\in S}\hat F_i \label{eq:milp-objective}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&\hat F_i\ge
\alpha^i_{p_i,b_i}p_{i,T'}
+\beta^i_{p_i,b_i}b_{i,T'}
+\gamma^i_{p_i,b_i},
&&\forall (p_i,b_i)\in\Theta_i,\ i\in S.
\label{eq:eudf-linearization}
\end{align}

\begin{subequations}\label{eq:usable-inventory}
\begin{align}
&p_{i,1}
=p_i^0-\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,1,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{-}}
+\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,1,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{+}}
+\sum_{m\in R}g_{i,1,m},
&&\forall i\in S_0, \label{eq:usable-inventory-first}\\
&p_{i,t}
=p_{i,t-1}-\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{-}}
+\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{+}}
+\sum_{m\in R}g_{i,t,m},
&&\forall i\in S_0,\ t=2,\ldots,T'. \label{eq:usable-inventory-recursive}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:broken-inventory}
\begin{align}
&b_{i,1}
=b_i^0-\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,1,k}^{\mathrm{b}^{-}}
+\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,1,k}^{\mathrm{b}^{+}}
-\sum_{m\in R}g_{i,1,m},
&&\forall i\in S_0, \label{eq:broken-inventory-first}\\
&b_{i,t}
=b_{i,t-1}-\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{b}^{-}}
+\sum_{k\in K}y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{b}^{+}}
-\sum_{m\in R}g_{i,t,m},
&&\forall i\in S_0,\ t=2,\ldots,T'. \label{eq:broken-inventory-recursive}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
\begin{equation}
p_{i,t}+b_{i,t}\le C_i,
\quad \forall i\in S,\ t=1,\ldots,T'.
\label{eq:station-capacity}
\end{equation}
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:usable-load}
\begin{align}
&\sum_{j\in S_0}\psi_{0,j,1,k}^{\mathrm{u}}
=y_{0,1,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{-}},
&&\forall k\in K, \label{eq:usable-load-initial}\\
&\sum_{j\in S_0}\psi_{i,j,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}}
=\sum_{\substack{j\in S_0:\\ t>t'_{j,i}}}
\psi_{j,i,t-t'_{j,i},k}^{\mathrm{u}}
-y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{+}}
+y_{i,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}^{-}},
&&\forall i\in S_0,\ t=2,\ldots,T',\ k\in K, \label{eq:usable-load-balance}\\
&\psi_{i,j,T',k}^{\mathrm{u}}
=0,
&&\forall i,j\in S_0,\ k\in K, \label{eq:usable-load-final}\\
&\psi_{i,j,t,k}^{\mathrm{u}}
\le Q_k x_{i,j,t,k}^{\mathrm{v}},
&&\forall i,j\in S_0,\ t=1,\ldots,T',\ k\in K. \label{eq:usable-load-link}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:broken-load}
\begin{align}
&\psi_{i,j,1,k}^{\mathrm{b}}
=0,
&&\forall i,j\in S_0,\ k\in K, \label{eq:broken-load-initial}\\
&\sum_{j\in S_0}\psi_{i,j,t,k}^{\mathrm{b}}
=\sum_{\substack{j\in S_0:\\ t>t'_{j,i}}}
\psi_{j,i,t-t'_{j,i},k}^{\mathrm{b}}

The objective in~\eqref{eq:milp-objective} minimizes total user dissatisfaction
at all stations on the following day. Constraints~\eqref{eq:eudf-linearization}
introduce the auxiliary variable $\hat F_i$ for each station $i$ and ensure that
it is no smaller than every fitted plane used to approximate the EUDF value at
the inventories after repositioning.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:usable-inventory-first} and
\eqref{eq:usable-inventory-recursive} keep track of the number of usable bikes at
each node at the end of each period. Usable-bike inventory decreases when trucks
collect usable bikes, increases when trucks deliver usable bikes, and also
increases when broken bikes are repaired on site. Constraints
\eqref{eq:broken-inventory-first} and~\eqref{eq:broken-inventory-recursive} keep
track of the number of broken bikes at each node at the end of each period.
Broken-bike inventory decreases when trucks collect broken bikes or repairers
repair broken bikes on site, and it increases when trucks unload broken bikes.
Constraints~\eqref{eq:station-capacity} stipulate that the total number of
usable and broken bikes at each station is no more than its capacity.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:usable-load-initial} specify the initial load of usable
bikes on each truck during the first period. Constraints
\eqref{eq:usable-load-balance} update the load of usable bikes on each truck at
each node during each period. Constraints~\eqref{eq:usable-load-final} require
that each truck carries no usable bikes at the end of the last period.
Constraints~\eqref{eq:usable-load-link} ensure that usable bikes can be carried
only on arcs traveled by trucks and that the usable-bike load does not exceed the
truck capacity. Similar constraints for broken bikes are provided in
constraints~\eqref{eq:broken-load-initial}--\eqref{eq:broken-load-link}.
Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-capacity} ensure that the total number of usable and
broken bikes on each truck during each period is no more than its capacity.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:usable-delivery-link} and
\eqref{eq:usable-pickup-link} make sure that the quantity of usable bikes
delivered to or collected from a visited node in a period is no more than the
minimum of the node capacity and the truck capacity. Constraints
\eqref{eq:broken-depot-delivery-link} limit the number of broken bikes that can
be deposited at the depot in a period by a truck. Constraints
\eqref{eq:no-broken-station-delivery} ensure that no broken bikes are delivered
to stations. Constraints~\eqref{eq:no-broken-depot-pickup} enforce that no
broken bikes are collected from the depot. Constraints
\eqref{eq:broken-station-pickup-link} make sure that the number of broken bikes
collected from a visited station in a period is no more than the minimum of that
station's initial broken-bike inventory and the truck capacity.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:no-depot-repair} guarantee that no broken bikes are
repaired by repairers at the depot. Constraints~\eqref{eq:repair-visit-link}
limit the number of broken bikes repaired at a station by a repairer in a period
and link on-site repair quantities to repairer visits. Constraints
\eqref{eq:broken-handling-limit} ensure that the total number of broken bikes
collected by trucks and repaired by repairers at each station does not exceed
that station's initial broken-bike inventory.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-start} and~\eqref{eq:repairer-start} require all
trucks and repairers to depart from the depot initially. Constraints
\eqref{eq:truck-return} and~\eqref{eq:repairer-return} require all trucks and
repairers to return to the depot by the end of the rebalancing horizon.
Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-flow} and~\eqref{eq:repairer-flow} are the flow
conservation conditions for trucks and repairers, respectively, and account for
the discrete travel duration on each arc. Constraints
\eqref{eq:truck-stay-lower}--\eqref{eq:repairer-stay-upper} relate
$\varsigma_{t,k}^{\mathrm{v}}$ and $\varsigma_{t,m}^{\mathrm{r}}$ to
$\partial_k^{\mathrm{v}}$ and $\partial_m^{\mathrm{r}}$, respectively, thereby
identifying the periods after a truck or repairer has made its final return to
the depot and remains there.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-time-upper} and~\eqref{eq:truck-time-lower}
restrict the upper and lower bounds of the cumulative operational time for each
truck. Specifically, constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-time-upper} imply that, up to
each period, the sum of truck travel time and loading or unloading time cannot
exceed the time elapsed from the start of repositioning to the end of that
period. Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-time-lower} imply that, up to each period
before the truck enters its final depot-stay portion, the same cumulative time
cannot be lower than the time elapsed to the end of two periods before that
period. Similar constraints are imposed on repairers in
constraints~\eqref{eq:repairer-time-upper} and~\eqref{eq:repairer-time-lower},
where cumulative operational time includes repairer travel time and on-site
repair time.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-depot-wait} and
\eqref{eq:repairer-depot-wait} ensure that a truck or repairer stays at the
depot after its final return. Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-self-loop} and
\eqref{eq:repairer-self-loop} require that, before the final return to the
depot, a truck or repairer that stays at the same node for another period must
conduct at least one loading, unloading, or repair activity there. Constraints
\eqref{eq:truck-one-action} enforce that each truck can travel between at most
one pair of nodes in a period. Similar constraints for repairers are given in
constraints~\eqref{eq:repairer-one-action}. Constraints
\eqref{eq:single-repairer-visit} ensure that each station can be visited at most
once by at most one repairer during the whole rebalancing horizon.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-route-domain} and
\eqref{eq:repairer-route-domain} define the binary route variables for trucks
and repairers. Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-stay-domain} and
\eqref{eq:repairer-stay-domain} define the binary depot-stay variables.
Constraints~\eqref{eq:inventory-domain} define the nonnegative inventory
variables. Constraints~\eqref{eq:action-domain} and~\eqref{eq:repair-domain}
define the nonnegative integer loading, unloading, collection, delivery, and
repair variables. Constraints~\eqref{eq:truck-return-domain} and
\eqref{eq:repairer-return-domain} define the return-period variables and their
bounds. Constraints~\eqref{eq:load-domain} define the nonnegative truck-load
variables, and constraints~\eqref{eq:eudf-domain-variable} define the
nonnegative auxiliary EUDF variables.

The formulation specifies the BRP-BR-OSR feasible set and the EUDF-based
objective on inventories after repositioning used as the reference problem in the
remainder of the paper.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}\label{sec:framework}\label{sec:algorithm}\label{sec:cfrs-framework}

This section presents the cluster-first route-second (CFRS) solution method for
large-scale BRP-BR-OSR instances. CFRS decomposes the original network into two
connected layers. In Stage A, the station set is partitioned into clusters whose
internal inventory-adjustment potential is high. In Stage B, each selected
cluster is converted into a cluster-level BRP-BR-OSR instance and solved with the
HGSADC-SBC algorithm of \citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}.

The partition is the interface between the two layers. The partition search
evaluates candidate partitions by Extended Activated Transfer Selection (EATS),
a fast surrogate that estimates the EUDF reduction obtainable inside each
cluster. The routing layer receives the best-found partition and produces the
actual on-site repair and repositioning routes. The remainder of this section
first presents the HGS-EUC partition search, including the EATS evaluation
routine, and then describes the routing interface.

\subsection{Clustering by HGS-EUC with EATS}\label{sec:hgs-euc}

HGS-EUC is a hybrid genetic search for EUDF-guided clustering. It maintains a
population of partition solutions, generates offspring through cluster-based
crossover, restores cluster-size feasibility, improves solutions through local
search education, and manages the population according to objective quality and
partition diversity. Algorithm~\ref{alg:hgs-euc} summarizes the procedure. The
solution representation, evaluation routine, construction method, search
operators, and population management rules are defined in the following
subsections.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{HGS-EUC partition search}
\label{alg:hgs-euc}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Station set $S$, number of clusters $M$, construction dissimilarity
$d^{\mathrm{con}}$, EATS value $G(\cdot)$, and HGS parameters
\Ensure Best-found partition $\Pi^\star$
\State $\mathcal P\gets\emptyset$; $\Pi^\star\gets\emptyset$;
$it_{\mathrm{noimp}}\gets 0$
\While{$|\mathcal P|<4\mu$}
    \State Construct a k-medoids solution $\Pi$ using $d^{\mathrm{con}}$
    \State Repair cluster sizes, educate $\Pi$, evaluate
    $S_{\mathrm{part}}(\Pi)$, and insert $\Pi$ into $\mathcal P$
    \State Update $\Pi^\star$
\EndWhile
\While{the stopping criteria are unsatisfied}
    \State Select two parents from $\mathcal P$ by tournament on biased fitness
    \State Generate two offspring by cluster-based crossover with swapped parent order
    \ForAll{offspring $\Omega$}
        \State Repair cluster sizes, educate $\Omega$, evaluate
        $S_{\mathrm{part}}(\Omega)$, and insert $\Omega$ into $\mathcal P$
    \EndFor
    \If{$|\mathcal P|>\mu+\lambda$}
        \State Apply survivor selection based on biased fitness
    \EndIf
    \State Update $\Pi^\star$ and $it_{\mathrm{noimp}}$
    \If{the diversification interval is reached}
        \State Retain elite solutions and regenerate additional solutions
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\State \Return $\Pi^\star$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Solution representation}\label{sec:hgs-euc-representation}

Let $S$ be the set of stations and let node $0$ denote the depot. HGS-EUC
represents a solution as a partition
\begin{equation}
\Pi=\{B_1,\ldots,B_M\},
\qquad
B_m\subseteq S,
\quad
B_m\cap B_{m'}=\emptyset\ (m\ne m'),
\quad
\bigcup_{m=1}^{M}B_m=S.
\label{eq:cfrs-partition}
\end{equation}
Each block $B_m$ is a station cluster. The solution stores the cluster
membership of every station and one representative station, called the medoid,
for each non-empty cluster. The medoid is an auxiliary representative of the
cluster, not an additional decision variable of the original BRP-BR-OSR model.
The initial bivariate station states $(p_i^0,b_i^0)$ are fixed instance data and
are not part of the solution representation.

The partition must satisfy cluster-size bounds
\begin{equation}
L\le |B_m|\le U,
\qquad m=1,\ldots,M,
\label{eq:partition-size-bounds}
\end{equation}
where $L$ and $U$ are the lower and upper bounds on the number of stations in a
cluster.

\subsubsection{Solution evaluation}\label{sec:hgs-euc-evaluation}

For a partition $\Pi=\{B_1,\ldots,B_M\}$, the search uses the score
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{part}}(\Pi)=\sum_{m=1}^{M}G(B_m),
\label{eq:partition-score}
\end{equation}
where $G(B_m)$ is the EATS value of cluster $B_m$. A larger
$S_{\mathrm{part}}(\Pi)$ indicates a partition with larger estimated EUDF
reduction under the cluster-level approximation.

EATS evaluates one cluster without constructing detailed vehicle or repairer
routes. Its core idea is to greedily activate local inventory-changing operations
that provide positive EUDF reduction per unit of proxy time, while maintaining
inventory feasibility and a cluster time budget. The accepted operations define
aggregate bivariate state changes, and the cluster value is the resulting EUDF
reduction.

The operation records considered by EATS have three types: a usable-bike transfer
from station $i$ to station $j$, a broken-bike pickup at station $i$ by a truck,
and an on-site repair at station $i$ by a repairer. An accepted record contains
the operation type and an accepted quantity $q$. These records imply aggregate
state changes $(\delta_i^p,\delta_i^b)$. A usable-bike transfer from $i$ to $j$
decreases $\delta_i^p$ by $q$ and increases $\delta_j^p$ by $q$. A broken-bike
pickup at $i$ decreases $\delta_i^b$ by $q$. An on-site repair at $i$ increases
$\delta_i^p$ by $q$ and decreases $\delta_i^b$ by $q$. After the accepted records
are fixed, the cluster value is
\begin{equation}
G(B)=
\sum_{i\in B}
\left[
F_i(p_i^0,b_i^0)
-F_i(p_i^0+\delta_i^p,b_i^0+\delta_i^b)
\right].
\label{eq:cluster-eats-value}
\end{equation}

The single-unit scores measure marginal EUDF reduction per unit of proxy
operation time. At a working state $(p_i,b_i)$, the marginal EUDF reduction from
removing one usable bike from station $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_i^{-}(p_i,b_i)=F_i(p_i,b_i)-F_i(p_i-1,b_i),
\label{eq:eats-remove-usable-marginal}
\end{equation}
and the marginal EUDF reduction from adding one usable bike to station $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_i^{+}(p_i,b_i)=F_i(p_i,b_i)-F_i(p_i+1,b_i).
\label{eq:eats-add-usable-marginal}
\end{equation}
For a usable-bike transfer from $i$ to $j$, the single-unit score is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{ij}^{\mathrm U}
=\frac{\Delta_i^{-}(p_i,b_i)+\Delta_j^{+}(p_j,b_j)}
{2t^{\mathrm{load}}}.
\label{eq:eats-usable-score}
\end{equation}
For a broken-bike pickup at station $i$, the single-unit score is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_i^{\mathrm B}
=\frac{F_i(p_i,b_i)-F_i(p_i,b_i-1)}{2t^{\mathrm{load}}}.
\label{eq:eats-broken-pickup-score}
\end{equation}
For an on-site repair at station $i$, the single-unit score is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_i^{\mathrm R}
=\frac{F_i(p_i,b_i)-F_i(p_i+1,b_i-1)}{t^{\mathrm{repair}}}.
\label{eq:eats-repair-score}
\end{equation}
Candidate operations are generated only when the resulting bivariate state is
feasible and the corresponding score is positive.

The time proxy separates truck work and repairer work into two parallel streams.
Let $A_T$ be the set of stations activated by accepted usable-bike transfers or
broken-bike pickups, and let $A_R$ be the set of stations activated by accepted
on-site repairs. Let $Q_U$, $Q_B$, and $Q_R$ be the accepted quantities of
usable-bike transfers, broken-bike pickups, and on-site repairs, respectively.
The cluster time proxy is
\begin{equation}
\widehat T(B)=\max\{\widehat T_T(B),\widehat T_R(B)\},
\label{eq:eats-cluster-time}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\widehat T_T(B)
=\operatorname{MSS}(A_T)
+2\operatorname{Depot}(A_T)
+2t^{\mathrm{load}}(Q_U+Q_B),
\label{eq:eats-truck-time}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\widehat T_R(B)
=\rho\left[
\operatorname{MSS}(A_R)+2\operatorname{Depot}(A_R)
\right]
+t^{\mathrm{repair}}Q_R.
\label{eq:eats-repairer-time}
\end{equation}
For any active set $A\subseteq B$, define
\begin{equation}
\bar t_{ij}=\max\{t_{ij}^{\mathrm v},t_{ji}^{\mathrm v}\},
\qquad i,j\in S_0,
\label{eq:eats-symmetric-time}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{align}
\operatorname{MSS}(A)
&=\max_{h\in A}\sum_{i\in A\setminus\{h\}}\bar t_{hi},
\label{eq:eats-mss}\\
\operatorname{Depot}(A)
&=\max_{i\in A}\max\{t_{0i}^{\mathrm v},t_{i0}^{\mathrm v}\}.
\label{eq:eats-depot-bound}
\end{align}
For $A=\emptyset$, both terms are set to zero. A candidate batch is admissible
only if the updated active sets and accepted quantities satisfy
\begin{equation}
\widehat T(B)\le T.
\label{eq:eats-time-budget}
\end{equation}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:eats} gives the complete EATS evaluation routine.

\begin{breakablealgorithm}
\caption{EATS evaluation of a cluster}
\label{alg:eats}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Cluster $B$, initial states $(p_i^0,b_i^0)$, EUDF functions $F_i(\cdot)$,
travel-time data, time budget $T$, loading time $t^{\mathrm{load}}$, repair time
$t^{\mathrm{repair}}$, and repairer travel-time multiplier $\rho$
\Ensure Cluster value $G(B)$ and accepted operation records $\mathcal R_B$
\Statex \textbf{Step 0. Initialization}
\State Set $(p_i,b_i)\gets(p_i^0,b_i^0)$ and $(\delta_i^p,\delta_i^b)\gets(0,0)$
for all $i\in B$
\State Set $\mathcal R_B\gets\emptyset$, $A_T\gets\emptyset$, $A_R\gets\emptyset$,
and $Q_U,Q_B,Q_R\gets0$
\State Initialize station-role flags and residual inventory counters
\Statex \textbf{Step 1. Candidate generation and scoring}
\State Generate feasible usable-bike transfer, broken-bike pickup, and on-site
repair candidates under the current working state
\State Compute their single-unit scores by
\eqref{eq:eats-usable-score}--\eqref{eq:eats-repair-score}
\State Insert positive-score candidates into a unified priority queue
\Statex \textbf{Step 2. Greedy activation}
\While{the priority queue is non-empty}
    \State Pop the candidate with the largest stored score
    \State Recheck feasibility under the current working state and station-role flags
    \State Recompute its single-unit score if the working state has changed
    \If{the recomputed score is positive}
        \State Find the largest admissible batch quantity $q$ that respects residual
        inventory, station-role consistency, and \eqref{eq:eats-time-budget}
        \If{$q>0$}
            \State Accept the batch, append the operation record to $\mathcal R_B$,
            and update $(p_i,b_i)$ and $(\delta_i^p,\delta_i^b)$ at affected stations
            \State Update $A_T$, $A_R$, $Q_U$, $Q_B$, $Q_R$, residual inventory
            counters, and affected candidate scores
        \EndIf
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\Statex \textbf{Step 3. Residual improvement}
\While{a positive-score usable-bike transfer or on-site repair is feasible}
    \State Select the best remaining candidate after lazy re-evaluation
    \State Accept the largest admissible batch quantity and update all affected states,
    records, active sets, quantities, and candidate scores
\EndWhile
\Statex \textbf{Step 4. Cluster valuation}
\State Compute $G(B)$ by \eqref{eq:cluster-eats-value}
\State \Return $G(B)$ and $\mathcal R_B$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{breakablealgorithm}

During EATS evaluation, station-level consistency is enforced throughout the two
selection phases. Usable-bike export and import roles are controlled by the
$p$-direction state of a station. Broken-bike pickup and on-site repair draw
from the same station-level broken-bike inventory, so their accepted quantities
at a station are jointly bounded by the available broken bikes. This keeps
\eqref{eq:cluster-eats-value} consistent with the aggregate bivariate state
changes returned by Algorithm~\ref{alg:eats}.

\subsubsection{Solution construction}\label{sec:hgs-euc-construction}

Initial and regenerated solutions are produced by a k-medoids-style construction.
The construction uses an auxiliary station-to-station dissimilarity matrix. For
two stations $i,j\in S$, let $\hat t_{ij}$ be the normalized symmetric truck
travel time and let $\hat P_{ij}$ be the normalized bilateral usable-transfer
potential computed from the initial bivariate states. The construction
dissimilarity is
\begin{equation}
d_{ij}^{\mathrm{con}}
=\alpha \hat t_{ij}+(1-\alpha)(1-\hat P_{ij}),
\qquad i,j\in S,
\label{eq:construction-dissimilarity}
\end{equation}
where $0\le \alpha\le 1$. A larger $\alpha$ gives more weight to travel-time
proximity, whereas a smaller $\alpha$ gives more weight to usable-transfer
potential.

The first medoid is selected randomly. Subsequent medoids are sampled with
probability increasing in each station's dissimilarity to its nearest current
medoid. After medoids are initialized, each station is assigned to the nearest
medoid, medoids are recomputed from the assigned clusters, and the assignment and
medoid-update steps are repeated until the medoids stabilize or an iteration cap
is reached. The constructed solution is then processed according to the
initialization and diversification steps in Algorithm~\ref{alg:hgs-euc}.

\subsubsection{Cluster-based crossover}\label{sec:hgs-euc-crossover}

Given two parents $\Pi^{(1)}=\{B_1^{(1)},\ldots,B_M^{(1)}\}$ and
$\Pi^{(2)}=\{B_1^{(2)},\ldots,B_M^{(2)}\}$, crossover copies a randomly selected
half of the clusters from $\Pi^{(1)}$ into the offspring. For each station that
remains unassigned, the operator reads its cluster assignment in $\Pi^{(2)}$. If
that cluster is not already affected by the inherited clusters from
$\Pi^{(1)}$, the station follows its assignment in $\Pi^{(2)}$. Otherwise, the
station is assigned to the inherited cluster with the nearest medoid under
$d^{\mathrm{con}}$. Empty clusters are reseeded and medoids are recomputed at the
end of the operator. Each mating event is applied with both parent orders, which
produces two offspring.

\subsubsection{Cluster-size repair and education}\label{sec:hgs-euc-repair-education}

Cluster-size repair is applied before education in both population initialization
and the evolutionary loop. Oversized clusters are reduced by relocating stations
to clusters with available capacity, and undersized clusters are filled by
pulling stations from clusters above the lower bound. Medoids are updated after
repair.

Education improves a repaired solution through local search. The education
module contains six operators: relocating one station, swapping two stations,
merge-splitting two clusters, relocating a pair of stations, swapping a pair for
a single station, and swapping one pair of stations for another pair. The
operators compare candidate moves by recomputing the EATS scores of the affected
clusters. In first-improvement mode, the operator order is randomized and the
procedure stops after the first improving move. In exhaustive mode, the operator
sequence is repeated until no operator yields further improvement.

\subsubsection{Population management and diversification}\label{sec:hgs-euc-population}

Population management balances objective value and partition diversity. Since
the search maximizes $S_{\mathrm{part}}(\Pi)$, objective ranks are assigned in
descending order of this score. Diversity is measured by pairwise same-cluster
disagreement. For two solutions $\Pi$ and $\Pi'$, define
\begin{equation}
D(\Pi,\Pi')=
\frac{2}{|S|(|S|-1)}
\sum_{\{i,j\}\subseteq S}
\left|\chi_{ij}(\Pi)-\chi_{ij}(\Pi')\right|,
\label{eq:partition-disagreement}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\chi_{ij}(\Pi)=
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if stations } i \text{ and } j \text{ are in the same cluster in } \Pi,\\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:same-cluster-indicator}
\end{equation}
The diversity rank is computed from the average disagreement to the
$n_{\mathrm{close}}$ closest solutions in the population. The biased fitness of a
solution is
\begin{equation}
BF(\Pi)=\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{obj}}(\Pi)+
\left(1-\frac{n_{\mathrm{elite}}}{|\mathcal P|}\right)
\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{div}}(\Pi).
\label{eq:biased-fitness-hgs-euc}
\end{equation}
Lower biased fitness is preferred in parent selection and survivor selection.
When the population size exceeds $\mu+\lambda$, solutions with the worst biased
fitness values are removed. Diversification is triggered after a prescribed
number of consecutive non-improving iterations: an elite subset is retained, and
additional solutions are generated by the construction procedure to restore
population diversity. HGS-EUC terminates when the no-improvement limit,
wall-clock limit, or maximum iteration limit is reached.

\subsection{Routing over selected clusters}\label{sec:routing-interface}

The routing layer receives the best-found partition
$\Pi^\star=\{B_1^\star,\ldots,B_M^\star\}$. For each selected cluster
$B_m^\star$, the adapter constructs the cluster-level node set
\begin{equation}
V_m^0=B_m^\star\cup\{0\},
\label{eq:cfrs-cluster-node-set}
\end{equation}
where node $0$ is the shared depot of the original network. The adapter reindexes
the stations in $B_m^\star$ as local station nodes, extracts the corresponding
travel-time, station-state, capacity, and EUDF data from the full instance, and
forms an independent cluster-level BRP-BR-OSR instance.

Each cluster-level instance is solved with HGSADC-SBC following
\citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}. Let $Z^{\mathrm{dissat}}(B_m^\star)$
be the total user dissatisfaction returned for cluster $B_m^\star$. The CFRS
route-level dissatisfaction is
\begin{equation}
Z_{\mathrm{CFRS}}^{\mathrm{dissat}}
=\sum_{m=1}^{M}Z^{\mathrm{dissat}}(B_m^\star).
\label{eq:cfrs-total-dissat}
\end{equation}
This value reports the outcome of the complete CFRS solution after the
cluster-level routing instances have been solved.

\section{Computational experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

The proposed CFRS method was implemented in C++20 and controlled by a Python
orchestrator. The experiments were carried out on two Linux workstations: a 13th
Gen Intel Core i9-13900 workstation with 32 logical cores and 62~GiB RAM, and a
12th Gen Intel Core i9-12900 workstation with 24 logical cores and 62~GiB RAM.
Method~A uses Gurobi~13.0 to solve the time-indexed formulation in
Section~\ref{sec:milp}; Methods~B and~C use the HGSADC-SBC implementation of
\citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}, with Method~C applying the CFRS
decomposition proposed in Section~\ref{sec:methodology} before solving the
cluster-level routing instances.

The test instances were prepared as follows. For network sizes up to 500
stations, we use the instances of \citet{huRepositioningBikeSharing2025}, which
are derived from the Citi Bike instances of \citet{hoHybridLargeNeighborhood2017}.
For the 800-, 1000-, and 2000-station instances, we generate additional
instances by sampling stations from a 2117-station Citi Bike pool covering
Manhattan and Brooklyn and by recomputing asymmetric station-to-station travel
times with OSRM driving routes. In all instances, the station capacities,
initial usable-bike inventories, initial broken-bike inventories, truck travel
times, and EUDF values are treated as input data. The EUDF values are computed
from historical rental and return rates following
\citet{kaspiBikesharingSystemsUser2017}.

The computational study uses one 60-station calibration instance and three
instance groups. The calibration instance is used only for parameter tuning.
Set~2 contains five instances at each of the 60-, 200-, and 300-station scales
and is used for the three-method benchmark. Set~3 contains five instances at
each of the 400-, 500-, 800-, 1000-, and 2000-station scales and is used for the
large-scale comparison. Set~4 contains additional configurations generated from
the Set~3 instances by varying one input parameter at a time: truck capacity,
operational time budget, target cluster size, and broken-bike rate.

Unless otherwise stated, the truck capacity is $Q=25$ bikes, the loading or
unloading time is $t^{\mathrm{load}}=60$~s per bike, the on-site repair time is
$t^{\mathrm{repair}}=300$~s per bike, and the operational time budget is
$T=7200$~s. All method runs are given a 600~s computational limit. Each
heuristic cell is replicated with 20 independent random seeds. For Method~A, we
report Gurobi's lower bound, upper bound, and MIP gap. For Methods~B and~C, we
report the average and best objective values over the 20 seeds. All objectives
are total user dissatisfaction values and are minimized. CPU time is therefore
not included as a comparison column.

The gap columns compare feasible objective values. Their definitions are given
in the table notes. A positive gap means that Method~C obtains a lower average
objective than the reference method.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper proposes an EUDF-aware cluster-first route-second heuristic to solve
the static bike-repositioning problem with broken bikes and on-site repair. In
this problem, trucks move usable bikes and collect broken bikes, while
repairers visit stations to repair broken bikes on site. The objective is to
minimize the total user dissatisfaction on the following day, evaluated by the
Extended User Dissatisfaction Function. The proposed heuristic first applies a
hybrid genetic search to partition the station network into clusters. An
Extended Activated Transfer Selection procedure is used to estimate the
user-dissatisfaction reduction within each cluster, and the resulting
cluster-level routing problems are solved by the HGSADC-SBC heuristic. To
illustrate the proposed heuristic's performance, it was tested on Citi
Bike-based instances with up to 2000 stations. On the small benchmark
instances, Gurobi does not close the MIP gap within the 600~s limit, and the
proposed heuristic improves the Gurobi upper bounds by 32.94\% on average. The
average improvement over the monolithic HGSADC-SBC heuristic is 7.26\% on the
small instances and 8.81\% on the large instances. For the five
2000-station instances, the average improvement is 11.71\%. The computational
results also show that the proposed heuristic maintains a positive advantage
under different vehicle capacities, time budgets, and broken-bike rates.
However, the heuristic fixes the station partition before detailed routing, so
it may miss useful operations involving stations in different clusters.
Therefore, a future research direction is to develop adaptive clustering and
cross-cluster coordination mechanisms for large-scale bike-repositioning
problems with broken bikes and on-site repair.
